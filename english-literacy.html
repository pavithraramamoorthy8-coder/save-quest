Grammar Nexus: The Sentence Core

1.1 Parts of Speech: Foundation

The English language is built on eight foundational categories, each determining a word's function within a sentence structure. Understanding these parts is crucial for syntactic mastery.

Nouns and Noun Phrases

Definition: Words that name a person, place, thing, or idea. They serve as the subject or object. Noun phrases can be simple (data) or complex (the large, complex data set).

Example: The engineer (N.) designed the algorithm (N.) for speed (N.).

Verbs and Predicates

Definition: Express actions, occurrences, or a state of being. The verb is the essential component of the predicate, telling us what the subject does or is.

Example: The system is running (V.) smoothly, and the developer fixed (V.) the bug.

Tense Quick Reference

2.1 Communicating Time in Technical Reports

Accurate verb tense is vital in professional and academic writing to clearly sequence events and convey causality.

TenseFormUsage Example
Simple Past(Verb + -ed)The team finished the project yesterday. (A definite end time)
Present Perfect(Has/Have + V3)I have seen this code before. (Action continuing to present)
Future Continuous(Will be + V-ing)Tomorrow, we will be coding all day. (Ongoing action in the future)

Use consistent tense throughout any single document or paragraph.

Writing Workshop: Structural Integrity

4.1 The T-E-C Paragraph Model

Achieve maximum clarity and logical flow by adhering to the Topic-Evidence-Conclusion (T-E-C) model for academic paragraphs.

  • Topic Sentence (T): States the paragraph's main argument.
  • Evidence/Explanation (E): Provides data, quotes, or reasoning to support the argument.
  • Concluding Sentence (C): Summarizes the idea and transitions to the next section.
